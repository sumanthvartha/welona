The big picture
Building a product is not
writing code.

Code is maybe 30% of the work. The other 70% is understanding, defining, designing, and validating. Most products fail not because the code is bad — but because they built the wrong thing beautifully.

"The most expensive mistake in product development is building something nobody asked for."

Stage 4 — You are here
Architect the Experience.

Before designing screens, you need to design the STRUCTURE. Where does every page live? How does the user flow from one to the next? What content needs to exist?

⏱ 1-2 weeks Can be done while Stage 5 starts in parallel

What you need to produce:

📐

4A. Sitemap

A visual tree of every page on the website and how they connect. This is the skeleton. Every page must have a clear purpose mapped to a user emotion from the research doc.

Pages to map:
Homepage → Branch Landing (×3) → Treatment Category → Treatment Detail (×5) → Quiz (6 steps) → Quiz Result → Compare Tool → Patient Stories → Doctor Profiles → Booking Flow → Confirmation

For each page, answer:
• What user emotion does this page serve?
• Where does the user come FROM?
• Where do they go NEXT?
• What's the ONE thing this page must accomplish?

4B. User Flows

Step-by-step paths for each key journey. Not just "user clicks button" — include the EMOTION at each step and what information they need to feel confident moving forward.

5 flows to map:
1. Discovery Flow: Google Ad → Land → See proof → Take quiz → Get recommendation → View treatment → Book
2. Research Flow: Land → Browse treatments → Compare → Read side effects → Check eligibility → Book
3. Trust Flow: Land → Watch video stories → Find "someone like me" → View their treatment → Book
4. Booking Flow: Select branch → Pick date → Pick time → Phone → OTP → Confirmed
5. Return Flow: Existing patient → Check progress → Book next session

4C. Content Inventory

List EVERY piece of content that needs to exist before you can build. This is often the longest lead time — you can't design a treatment page without the treatment data.

Content needed per treatment (×5 treatments):
• Plain-language description (what is it, how it works, 4 steps)
• Price range (min–max per session/total)
• Side effects (common, uncommon, rare — each with duration)
• Safety score + "honest truth" paragraph + "when it won't work"
• Eligibility criteria (3 yes/no questions)
• Expected timeline (month-by-month progress)
• Recommended number of sessions

Content needed per doctor (×4 doctors):
• Name, photo, qualification, specialization
• Years of experience, consultation count
• Languages spoken
• Treatments they perform

Content needed per branch (×3):
• Address, landmark, phone, hours
• Patient count, Google rating
• Local patient stories

Patient stories (minimum 6-8):
• Name (or initials), age, location, concern
• Treatment chosen, sessions, months to result
• Quote (their words, not yours)
• Video (for at least 3-4)
🚪 EXIT GATE — Stage 4

You can move to Stage 5 when you have: a complete sitemap, all 5 user flows mapped, and a content inventory with status (have it / need to create / need from clinic) for every item.

Stage 5
Design the Interface.

Now — and only now — do you open a design tool. You design with the architecture as your blueprint and the user research as your compass.

⏱ 2-3 weeks
✏️

5A. Wireframes (Low-Fidelity)

Grey boxes. No colors, no fonts, no images. Just LAYOUT and HIERARCHY. The wireframe answers: "What does the user see first? Second? Third?" This is where you decide that video testimonials go in the CENTER of the homepage — not tucked at the bottom.

Wireframe every unique screen:
Homepage · Branch page · Treatment detail · Quiz (1 screen, since layout repeats) · Compare tool · Stories page · Booking flow (3 screens) · Confirmation

For mobile AND desktop. 80% of your users are on phone. Design mobile first.
FigmaBalsamiqPaper sketches → photo
🎨

5B. Design System

Before designing pages, design the COMPONENTS. Colors, typography, buttons, cards, form inputs, badges. This ensures consistency and speeds up everything that follows.

Define:
• Color palette (primary, secondary, success, warning, danger, neutrals)
• Typography scale (headings 1-4, body, caption, label)
• Spacing system (4px base unit: 4, 8, 12, 16, 24, 32, 48, 64)
• Button styles (primary, secondary, text, disabled)
• Card styles (default, highlighted, interactive)
• Form elements (input, dropdown, slider, checkbox, radio)
• Badge/tag styles (safety scores, treatment categories)
• Icon style (emoji vs custom SVG — decide once)
💎

5C. High-Fidelity Mockups

Now add color, real content, real images. These should look like the FINAL product. Show them to 3-5 people who match your personas. Watch their faces — confusion means redesign.

🧪 Quick user test before moving on:
Show the homepage to 5 people. Ask: "What is this website for?" and "What would you do first?"
If they don't say "clinic" and "find treatment / book" within 5 seconds → redesign.
🚪 EXIT GATE — Stage 5

You can move to Stage 6 when: wireframes approved, design system documented, high-fidelity mockups for all key screens done, and 5-person hallway test passed.

Stage 6
Create the Content.

This is the stage everyone underestimates. You can't launch a treatment page without treatment data. You can't show patient stories without collecting them. Start this EARLY — it has the longest lead time.

⏱ 2-4 weeks Start in parallel with Stage 5

📋 Treatment Data Collection

Sit with Welona's doctors. For each treatment: get the REAL price ranges, REAL side effects, REAL session counts, REAL timelines. Don't copy from websites — get it from the doctors who do these procedures daily.

Who: You + clinic medical director · Time: 2-3 hours per treatment

📸 Patient Story Collection

Identify 6-8 satisfied patients. Ask them to share their experience in their own words. Record 3-4 video testimonials (even phone quality is fine — authenticity > production value). Get written consent.

Who: Clinic reception team identifies candidates · Time: 1-2 weeks to collect

👩‍⚕️ Doctor Profiles

Professional photos, verified credentials, specializations, languages, consultation counts. This content doesn't exist today — it's a critical trust gap.

Who: You coordinate with each doctor · Time: 1 week

✍️ Copy & SEO Content

Write all page copy, FAQ schema markup, meta descriptions, treatment descriptions in plain language. Remember: your user doesn't speak doctor. Write like you're explaining to a smart friend.

Who: You (or a content writer) · Time: 1-2 weeks
🚪 EXIT GATE — Stage 6

All treatment data verified by doctors, 6+ patient stories collected (3+ with video), all doctor profiles complete, all page copy written and reviewed.

Stage 8
Validate & Launch.

Don't launch to everyone on day one. Launch to one branch. Watch real users. Fix what breaks. Then expand.

⏱ 2-3 weeks

Week 1: Internal Testing + Usability

• Give the site to 5 real people who match your personas
• Ask them to: "Find a treatment for your concern and book a consultation"
• Watch them (screen share or in-person). Don't help. Just observe.
• Where do they get confused? Where do they pause? Where do they smile?
• Fix the confusion points. Ship fixes in 2-3 days.

Week 2: Beta Launch — T. Nagar Only

• Route T. Nagar Google Ads to the new site
• Keep other branches on the old site
• Monitor: bounce rate, quiz completion, booking rate, time-on-page
• Talk to reception: "Are people mentioning the website? Did they use the quiz?"
• Fix issues daily. This is the most important 2 weeks of the project.

Week 3: Full Rollout

• If T. Nagar metrics are positive → roll out to Adyar and Anna Nagar
• Switch all Google Ads to new site
• Redirect welona.in to new platform
• Announce on Google My Business, social media

Measure & Iterate.
Forever.

Launching is not the finish line. It's the starting line. Now you learn what's ACTUALLY working — because real users will always surprise you.

Quiz completion rate Target: >60%
Quiz → Booking rate Target: >25%
Homepage bounce rate Target: <40%

No-show rate Target: <15%

After 30 Days: Decide Phase 2

Look at the data. Which features are used most? Where do users drop off? What questions are they asking reception that the site doesn't answer? This data — not your assumptions — decides what to build next.

Rules to live by
7 things that will save you
from wasting months.

1. Never build a feature you can't trace back to the user research doc.

If it doesn't solve a pain point, fear, or job from that document — it doesn't ship. Period.

2. Ship ugly before shipping late.

A working product with real data and imperfect design beats a beautiful product that isn't live. You can always improve design. You can't improve a product nobody is using.

3. Content is the product. Code is just the delivery mechanism.

The real value is the treatment data, the honest side effects, the real patient stories. The React code just renders it. Don't spend 3 weeks on animations when the treatment descriptions are still lorem ipsum.

4. Test with 5 users, not 50.

Jakob Nielsen proved that 5 users find 85% of usability problems. Don't wait for a big study. Show it to 5 people who match your personas. Fix what confuses them. Repeat.

5. One branch first. Always.

Launch at T. Nagar. Learn for 2 weeks. Fix everything. Then expand. Multi-branch launch on day one = multi-branch disaster on day two.

6. Your opinion doesn't matter. The data does.

After launch, stop debating whether the quiz should be 4 steps or 6. Look at the completion rate. If 70% complete 6 steps, it's fine. If 30% drop at step 4, you have your answer.

7. The biggest risk isn't building the wrong feature. It's never launching.

Perfectionism kills more products than bad code. Set a launch date. Hit it. Fix everything after. The cost of inaction (₹30-40 lakhs/month in lost revenue from your diagnosis) is real and compounding.
